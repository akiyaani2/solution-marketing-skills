# Pressure Test — The Seven Lenses Framework

Each lens examines a different dimension of risk. Applied together, they create a comprehensive stress test that catches what individual reviews miss.

---

## Lens 1: Assumption Audit

**Why this lens matters:** The most dangerous risks are the ones you've assumed away. Plans fail not because of unknown unknowns, but because of untested certainties — things "everyone knows" that turn out to be wrong.

### How to apply:
1. Read through the plan and list every assumption (stated or implied)
2. For each assumption, rate:
   - **Confidence**: High / Medium / Low / Untested
   - **Impact if wrong**: Critical / Significant / Minor
   - **Evidence**: What data supports this assumption? Is it current?
3. Flag any assumption that is both **Low-confidence AND Critical-impact** — these are the plan's hidden landmines

### Output format:
| # | Assumption | Confidence | Impact If Wrong | Evidence |
|---|-----------|-----------|----------------|----------|
| 1 | [assumption] | [rating] | [rating] | [evidence or "none"] |

### Common hidden assumptions to check:
- "The team has capacity for this" (have you checked their current load?)
- "Leadership will approve the budget" (have you socialized the number?)
- "The timeline is realistic" (based on what precedent?)
- "The partner will deliver on time" (what's their track record?)
- "The audience will show up" (what's your registration-to-attendance ratio historically?)

## Lens 2: Resource Reality Check

**Why this lens matters:** Plans are built on paper where people have infinite bandwidth. Reality is that every team member is juggling multiple priorities, and the same "key person" shows up on every critical path. This lens forces an honest look at whether the humans can actually execute the plan.

### Questions to answer:
- Does this plan account for who actually does the work? (Not just "the team" — specific people)
- Are the same people assigned to multiple concurrent priorities?
- Is there slack for the unexpected? (Sick days, fire drills, partner delays)
- What happens if a key person is unavailable for 2 weeks?
- Are vendor/partner resources confirmed or assumed?
- Does this require new skills or tools the team doesn't have yet?

### Red flags:
- A single person is on the critical path for 3+ workstreams
- Plan assumes 100% capacity with no buffer
- Dependencies on people outside your control with no backup plan
- Timeline requires overtime or weekend work (unsustainable)

## Lens 3: Timeline Stress Test

**Why this lens matters:** Timelines are the first thing to break because they're built on optimistic assumptions stacked on top of each other. A 1-week slip in step 2 becomes a 3-week slip by step 5 because of cascading dependencies.

### Questions to answer:
- What are the hard deadlines (event dates, external launches) vs. soft targets?
- Which dependencies are sequential (must happen in order) vs. parallelizable?
- What's the critical path? What's the float?
- If the start slips 2 weeks, does the plan still work?
- Are there external dependencies with no SLA? (Partner reviews, legal approvals, vendor deliveries)
- What's the latest possible start date that still meets the deadline?

### The 2-Week Slip Test:
Take the plan's start date. Push it forward 2 weeks. Does the end date still work? If not, where does it break? That's your most fragile dependency.

## Lens 4: Stakeholder Blindspots

**Why this lens matters:** Plans fail politically as often as they fail operationally. A technically perfect plan that ignores a key stakeholder's concerns, skips an approval gate, or surprises someone with veto power will still fail.

### Questions to answer:
- Who needs to approve this that hasn't been consulted?
- Who will be affected that hasn't been informed?
- Who has veto power that isn't in the plan?
- What would your manager push back on? Your skip-level? The executive sponsor?
- Are there cross-team dependencies where the other team doesn't know they're a dependency?
- Is anyone going to feel blindsided when this launches?

### Stakeholder mapping exercise:
| Stakeholder | Role in This Plan | Current Awareness | Risk If Ignored |
|-------------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| [name/role] | Approver / Affected / Blocker | Informed / Unaware | [consequence] |

## Lens 5: Competitive Counter-Move

**Why this lens matters:** Plans are often built in a vacuum, assuming competitors stand still. But your competitors are making plans too. This lens forces you to think about whether your plan creates a durable advantage or just matches what the market is already doing.

### Questions to answer:
- If your competitor saw this plan, what would they do?
- Does this plan create a defensible advantage or just match the market?
- Is there a faster, cheaper, or more impactful alternative you haven't considered?
- What's your competitor doing in this space right now?
- Does this plan differentiate, or is it table stakes?
- Could a competitor replicate this in less time with fewer resources?

### Competitive framing:
- "We're doing X" -> "We're doing X, which competitors can't easily replicate because [moat]"
- "We're launching Y" -> "We're launching Y before [competitor] because [speed advantage]"

## Lens 6: Failure Modes

**Why this lens matters:** There are three types of failure: the likely kind, the catastrophic kind, and the sneaky kind. Most planning only addresses the first. This lens forces you to think about all three, including partial success scenarios where the plan "works" but doesn't deliver the intended value.

### Questions to answer:
- **Most likely failure:** What's the thing that's most probably going to go wrong? (Usually resource constraints or timeline slips)
- **Most catastrophic failure:** What's the worst-case scenario? (Budget blow-up, public embarrassment, partner relationship damage)
- **Sneaky failure:** What's the failure mode nobody's thinking about? (The quiet one that erodes value slowly — wrong audience, wrong metric, wrong framing)
- **Partial success:** If this partially succeeds, is partial success still valuable? Or is it all-or-nothing?

### Failure mode table:
| Mode | Type | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|------|-----------|--------|-----------|
| [description] | Likely / Catastrophic / Sneaky | H/M/L | H/M/L | [proposed fix] |

## Lens 7: Opportunity Cost

**Why this lens matters:** Every plan commits resources — people, budget, time, leadership attention. Those resources can't be used for something else. This lens asks whether this is truly the highest-value use of those resources, or if you're optimizing the wrong thing.

### Questions to answer:
- What are you NOT doing by committing to this?
- Is there something with higher ROI that this displaces?
- Could the same resources produce more impact elsewhere?
- Is this plan the best version of this idea, or are you locked into an early concept?
- If you had to cut this plan and redirect resources, where would they go?

### The "What else?" test:
List the top 3 things you could do instead with the same resources. If any of them clearly outperforms the current plan, you should seriously reconsider — or at minimum, be able to articulate why this plan is still the right choice.
