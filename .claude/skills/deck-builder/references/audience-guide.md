# Deck Builder — Audience Guide

Calibrate your deck to the audience. The same information presented to a VP and to a team member should look completely different — different depth, different framing, different slide count.

---

## Audience Presets

| Audience | Tone | Focus | Slide Count | Key Rule |
|----------|------|-------|-------------|----------|
| VP / Director | Strategic, metric-driven | Outcomes, risks, asks | 5-7 | Lead with impact. Every slide must answer "so what?" |
| Skip-level (VP+) | Executive, headline-only | Impact, alignment, decisions | 3-5 | If it takes more than 5 slides, you haven't distilled enough |
| Team members | Tactical, detail-rich | Tasks, timelines, owners | 8-12 | Include the "how" — they need to execute, not just understand |
| Partners | Professional, collaborative | Shared goals, joint metrics | 5-7 | Frame everything as mutual benefit, never one-sided asks |
| Peers (cross-team leads) | Casual, cross-team | Shared items, dependencies | 3-5 | Only include what affects them. Skip your internal details |

---

## Audience-Specific Adjustments

### VP / Director Audience
- **Do:** Use headline titles ("Registration Hit 150% of Target"), include competitive context, show business impact metrics
- **Don't:** Include task-level details, list individual contributors unless relevant, show process steps
- **Slide structure:** Title -> Executive Summary -> 2-3 content slides -> Ask -> Appendix
- **Why this works:** At this level, they're reading 10+ decks a week. Yours must be scannable in under 2 minutes. They need enough to make a decision, not enough to execute.

### Skip-Level (VP+) Audience
- **Do:** Lead with portfolio-level impact, include strategic alignment to company priorities, frame competitively
- **Don't:** Go below program level, include team names, show operational details
- **Slide structure:** Title -> 2 content slides max -> Ask (if any)
- **Why this works:** CVP+ audiences make decisions across a broad portfolio. They need to know if something is on track, at risk, or needs their attention — nothing more.

### Team Member Audience
- **Do:** Include specific owners, deadlines, task breakdowns, process details, links to resources
- **Don't:** Over-strategize, use executive framing that feels disconnected from their daily work
- **Slide structure:** Whatever length serves clarity. 8-12 slides is fine.
- **Why this works:** Team members execute. They need the "what," "how," "when," and "who" — not the "why does this matter to the CEO."

### Partner Audience
- **Do:** Frame as joint achievement, show shared metrics, highlight mutual benefit, use professional but warm tone
- **Don't:** Share internal challenges, show one-sided asks, include internal org details
- **Slide structure:** Title -> Shared context -> Results/Progress -> Joint next steps -> Ask
- **Why this works:** Partners need to feel invested, not used. Every slide should reinforce "we're in this together."

### Peer (Cross-Team) Audience
- **Do:** Focus on dependencies, shared timelines, resource conflicts, coordination points
- **Don't:** Include anything that only matters to your team internally
- **Slide structure:** Title -> Shared context -> What affects them -> Ask/Coordination needed
- **Why this works:** Peers are busy with their own priorities. Respect their time by making every slide directly relevant to them.

---

## The "So What" Test

For every slide, regardless of audience, ask: **"So what?"**

- "We had 400 signups" -> So what? -> "400 signups in 48 hours, 2x pace vs. last year, putting us on track to exceed annual target"
- "Content is in review" -> So what? -> "Content lock confirmed for May 15, on schedule for Build launch"
- "Budget is at 60% spent" -> So what? -> "60% spent with 70% of programs delivered — tracking to finish under budget"

If you can't answer "so what?" — the slide doesn't belong in the deck.
