# Leadership Update — Altitude Check

The single most important skill for leadership updates is knowing what to include and what to leave out. This guide provides the altitude filter: the test that separates "leadership-level" from "team-level" content.

---

## The Include / Don't Include Table

| Include | Don't Include |
|---------|--------------|
| Strategic direction changes | Minor task completions |
| Program milestones with business impact | Individual ticket updates |
| Competitive positioning wins | Internal process changes |
| Partner milestones (co-marketing, joint launches) | Fire drills or temporary blockers already being handled |
| Metrics WITH business context | Raw numbers without narrative |
| Risks that affect direction | Risks already mitigated |
| Decisions made that shift priorities | Decisions about internal workflow |
| Cross-team dependencies or alignment | Work that only affects your team |

---

## The Altitude Test

Before including ANY bullet in a leadership update, apply this three-question filter:

### Question 1: "Would my skip-level care about this?"
If your VP's VP wouldn't mention this in their own update, it's too granular. Cut it.

### Question 2: "Does this connect to a business outcome?"
- "Updated 12 issues" -> No business outcome. Cut.
- "Hackathon registration hit 150% of target, putting us ahead of annual goal" -> Clear business outcome. Keep.

### Question 3: "Would this change anyone's decision?"
If knowing this information wouldn't cause leadership to act differently, it's informational noise, not a leadership update item.

**If a bullet fails any of these three questions, it doesn't belong in the leadership update.** Move it to a team standup or a 1:1 instead.

## Examples: Transforming Team-Level into Leadership-Level

### Example 1: Hackathon Progress

**Team-level (too granular):**
> - Closed 12 hackathon-related issues
> - Updated registration page content
> - Had planning call with partner team

**Leadership-level (right altitude):**
> **Hackathon registration pipeline on track for annual target**
> - Partner co-marketing registration opened; 400 signups in first 48 hours (2x pace vs prior year)
> - Unified platform confirmed for Q4 flagship event — first cross-team hackathon platform

### Example 2: Content Program

**Team-level (too granular):**
> - Published 3 blog posts
> - Reviewed 5 social media drafts
> - Updated content calendar

**Leadership-level (right altitude):**
> **Content engine hitting stride — 3 assets shipped with combined 15K views in week 1**
> - Flagship blog post drove 8K views (2x average), shared by 3 partner accounts organically

### Example 3: Event Planning

**Team-level (too granular):**
> - Confirmed AV vendor for conference booth
> - Sent session abstracts for review
> - Booked team travel

**Leadership-level (right altitude):**
> **Conference prep on track — all 4 sessions accepted, booth design in final review**
> - Confirmed keynote speaking slot (first time for our team at this event)

### Example 4: Risk Reporting

**Team-level (too much operational detail):**
> - Vendor missed deadline by 3 days
> - Had to re-do the deliverable formatting
> - Working extra hours to catch up

**Leadership-level (right altitude):**
> **Risk: Vendor delivery slip — mitigated by compressing internal review from 5 days to 3**
> - On track for original deadline; no downstream impact to partner launch

## The "Light Week" Protocol

Some weeks, there's nothing strategic to report. That's fine. Don't pad the update with granular items just to have content. Instead:

> "Steady execution this week — all programs tracking to plan. Key milestone: [next milestone] on [date]."

This is a perfectly valid leadership update. It tells leadership that nothing requires their attention, which is itself valuable information. A short update shows confidence and discipline. A padded update shows insecurity.

## Audience-Specific Altitude

| Audience | Altitude | What They Need |
|----------|----------|---------------|
| VP+ | Portfolio level | "Is the overall portfolio healthy? Any strategic shifts?" |
| Sr. Director | Program level | "Are key programs on track? What needs my attention?" |
| Director (your manager) | Initiative level | "What's moving, what's stuck, what decisions do you need from me?" |
| Peers | Dependency level | "What affects my team? What do I need to coordinate on?" |

Adjust the altitude of your bullets based on who's reading. The same accomplishment might be a full bullet for your Director, a sub-bullet for your VP, and not included at all for the CVP.
