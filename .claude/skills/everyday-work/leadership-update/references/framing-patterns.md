# Leadership Update — Framing Patterns

Every leadership update bullet needs a frame — a lens that makes the information meaningful. Raw facts don't land. Framed facts do. Here are the three framing patterns and when to use each.

---

## Pattern 1: Competitive Frame

**When to use:** When you have data showing your program outperforms alternatives, when competitors are making moves in your space, or when leadership is focused on market positioning.

**Structure:**
```
[What we did] — [how it compares to the competition or the market]
```

**Examples:**
> **Partner hackathon outpacing industry benchmarks**
> - 400 registrations in 48 hours — 2x the pace of [competitor]'s equivalent program last quarter
> - Co-marketing MDF investment yielding 3x the registration-per-dollar vs. standalone events

> **Content reach exceeding peer programs**
> - Flagship blog series averaging 8K views per post — top 5% performance across the marketing org
> - Organic partner amplification driving 30% of traffic (vs. 10% industry average)

**Why this works:** Leadership thinks in competitive terms. "We did well" is forgettable. "We outperformed the alternative by 2x" is memorable and quotable. Competitive framing also makes your work defensible — it's harder to cut a program that's outperforming the competition.

**Tips:**
- Compare to: competitor programs, prior year performance, industry benchmarks, peer team results
- Use specific ratios: "2x," "3x," "top 5%," "first time"
- If you don't have direct competitor data, compare to your own prior period or to internal benchmarks

---

## Pattern 2: Metric-Led Frame

**When to use:** When you have strong numbers that speak for themselves, when the audience is data-driven, or when you need to show progress against a target.

**Structure:**
```
[Metric] — [vs. target / vs. prior period] — [what it means]
```

**Examples:**
> **Registration pipeline at 85% of annual target with 2 quarters remaining**
> - 2,400 total registrations (target: 2,800) — tracking to exceed by 15% at current pace
> - Q3 conversion rate: 34% (vs. 28% target) — improved onboarding flow paying off

> **Event NPS holding strong at 72 (industry benchmark: 55)**
> - Third consecutive quarter above 70 — driven by pre-event content and partner co-delivery
> - Action: Scaling the pre-event content model to all Q4 events

**Why this works:** Numbers create precision and credibility. A metric-led frame says "I measure what matters and I'm tracking it." Leadership can quickly assess health: above target = good, below = needs attention. Always include the comparison point — a number without context is meaningless.

**Tips:**
- Every metric needs a comparison: vs. target, vs. last quarter, vs. last year, vs. benchmark
- Use "at X% of target" language — it's clearer than absolute numbers
- Include the trend direction: "up from," "maintaining," "down from"
- If a metric is below target, pair it with the mitigation

---

## Pattern 3: Strategic Frame

**When to use:** When the update relates to a larger strategic initiative, when you're connecting your work to company or org-level priorities, or when the "why" matters more than the "what."

**Structure:**
```
[Strategic initiative headline] — [how this week's progress advances it]
```

**Examples:**
> **Unified hackathon platform advancing cross-team strategy**
> - First cross-team event platform confirmed for Q4 — aligns with org-wide "One Platform" initiative
> - Three solution plays now sharing registration infrastructure (vs. 3 separate systems previously)

> **Partner co-marketing model proving scalable**
> - MDF-funded virtual format delivered 400 registrations at 1/3 the cost of in-person equivalent
> - Model now templated for reuse across Q4 partner programs — briefing cross-team peers next week

**Why this works:** Strategic framing connects your work to what leadership already cares about. If the org has a "platform unification" priority, showing your work advances it makes your team indispensable. It also shows you understand the bigger picture, not just your lane.

**Tips:**
- Reference specific company or org-level initiatives by name
- Show how your work is a proof point for the strategy, not just an independent effort
- Use language like "aligns with," "advances," "proof point for," "scales to"
- Connect to the audience's stated priorities (what did they say in the last all-hands?)

---

## Choosing the Right Frame

| Situation | Best Frame | Why |
|-----------|-----------|-----|
| You have a number that beats the target | Metric-Led | Let the data do the talking |
| You outperformed a competitor or peer | Competitive | Creates a memorable comparison |
| Your work connects to an org priority | Strategic | Shows alignment and big-picture thinking |
| You have all three | Lead with Competitive, support with Metric, close with Strategic | The triple frame is the strongest |
| You have none (light week) | Skip it | Better to have 2 strong bullets than 5 weak ones |

---

## Combining Frames

The strongest leadership update bullets combine two or three frames:

> **Hackathon registration outpacing all prior programs [COMPETITIVE] — 85% of annual target with 2 quarters remaining [METRIC] — validating the scalable partner co-marketing model [STRATEGIC]**

This single bullet tells leadership: we're winning, we're on track, and it connects to the strategy. That's the gold standard.

---

## Negative Item Framing

When reporting risks, blockers, or misses, always use this structure:

```
[Risk/Issue] — [Impact if unaddressed] — [Mitigation in progress]
```

**Never:**
> - Timeline slipped by a week
> - Vendor didn't deliver on time
> - We're behind on content

**Always:**
> - **Risk: Vendor delivery slip — mitigated by compressing review from 5 days to 3; original deadline preserved**
> - **Timeline adjusted: Content lock moved from May 10 to May 15 — no downstream impact to launch**

**Why:** Leadership wants to know you're managing the situation, not just reporting it. Negative items without mitigation create anxiety. Negative items with mitigation create confidence.
