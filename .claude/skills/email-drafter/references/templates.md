# Email Drafter — Email Templates

Five templates covering the most common professional email types. Choose the template that matches your situation, then customize for audience and tone using the tone guide.

---

## Template 1: Status Update (to leadership)

**Use when:** Providing a regular update on a program, initiative, or workstream. No action required from the reader.

```
Subject: [Program] Update — [Key Headline]

Hi [Name],

Quick update on [program]:

**Status:** [On track / At risk / Needs attention]

**Highlights:**
- [Win or milestone with metric]
- [Win or milestone with metric]

**Coming up:**
- [Next milestone] — [date]
- [Next milestone] — [date]

[Optional: 1-2 sentence risk or FYI — only if material]

Happy to discuss in our next sync or jump on a call if helpful.

Best,
[Your name]
```

**Why this structure works:** Leadership reads status updates to confirm things are on track. Lead with status (green/yellow/red), then evidence. They'll stop reading after "Highlights" if the status is green — that's fine. The "Coming up" section gives them something to watch for next time.

## Template 2: Ask / Approval Request

**Use when:** You need a decision, budget approval, resource allocation, or any action from the recipient.

```
Subject: [Action Needed] [Topic] — [Deadline if applicable]

Hi [Name],

I'm writing to request [specific approval/decision/resource].

**What:** [1-2 sentences on what you need]
**Why:** [1-2 sentences on why it matters — tie to business impact]
**Cost/Scope:** [budget, timeline, or resource ask]
**Deadline:** [when you need the decision by and why]

**My recommendation:** [What you think we should do]

I've attached [supporting doc/data] for reference. Happy to walk through this in our next 1:1 or on a quick call.

Thanks,
[Your name]
```

**Why this structure works:** The bold field labels make this scannable. An approver can skim What/Why/Cost/Deadline in 10 seconds and know exactly what they're being asked. The recommendation shows you've done the thinking — you're not dumping a problem on them.

## Template 3: Escalation

**Use when:** An issue exceeds your decision authority, impacts a stakeholder, or creates a timeline risk that leadership needs to know about.

```
Subject: [Escalation] [Issue] — Need Your Input by [Date]

Hi [Name],

I'm escalating [issue] because [reason — it's above my decision authority / it impacts a key stakeholder / timeline risk].

**Situation:** [2-3 sentences on what's happening]
**Options:**
1. [Option A] — [Pro/Con]
2. [Option B] — [Pro/Con]
**My recommendation:** [Option X] because [reasoning]
**Impact of delay:** [What happens if we don't decide by [date]]

Can we discuss this in [our next sync / a 15-min call this week]?

Thanks,
[Your name]
```

**Why this structure works:** Escalations must include your recommendation. Never send a "what should I do?" email to leadership. Show that you've analyzed the options, picked one, and explained why — then ask them to confirm or redirect. The "Impact of delay" creates appropriate urgency without being alarmist.

## Template 4: Partner Outreach

**Use when:** Reaching out to an external partner, vendor, or cross-company contact for the first time or proposing a new collaboration.

```
Subject: [Partnership Opportunity / Follow-up] — [Your Org] x [Their Org]

Hi [Name],

[1 sentence of context — how you know them, who referred you, or what prompted this outreach]

I'm reaching out because [specific opportunity/proposal]. [1-2 sentences on what you're proposing and why it benefits both sides].

**Proposed next step:** [Specific ask — a 30-min call, review of a doc, intro to someone]

Would [suggested time] work for a quick conversation?

Best,
[Your name]
[Title]
```

**Why this structure works:** Partners are busy and skeptical of cold outreach. The context sentence establishes credibility. The mutual-benefit framing makes it clear this isn't a one-sided ask. The specific proposed next step makes it easy to say yes — "sure, let's do a 30-min call" is much easier than "I'm interested, what do you propose?"

## Template 5: Follow-Up (after meeting or event)

**Use when:** Sending a recap after a meeting, call, event, or important conversation to align on takeaways and next steps.

```
Subject: Follow-up: [Meeting/Event Name] — [Date]

Hi [Name],

Thanks for [meeting/attending/presenting] [specific reference to what happened].

**Key takeaways:**
- [Takeaway 1]
- [Takeaway 2]

**Action items:**
- [You] will [action] by [date]
- [They] will [action] by [date]

**Next step:** [What happens next and when]

Let me know if I missed anything. Looking forward to [next touchpoint].

Best,
[Your name]
```

**Why this structure works:** Follow-up emails create accountability. By writing down the action items and owners, you ensure nothing falls through the cracks. The "Let me know if I missed anything" gives the other person a chance to correct the record — and creates implicit agreement if they don't.

## Template Selection Guide

| Situation | Template | Key Signal |
|-----------|----------|-----------|
| "Just keeping you posted" | Status Update | No action needed from reader |
| "I need you to approve/decide" | Ask / Approval | Reader must take action |
| "This is above my pay grade" | Escalation | Issue exceeds your authority |
| "Let's work together" | Partner Outreach | New or expanded relationship |
| "Here's what we agreed" | Follow-Up | Recap after a conversation |
