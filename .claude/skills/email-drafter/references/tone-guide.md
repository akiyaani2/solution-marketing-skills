# Email Drafter — Tone & Audience Calibration Guide

The same message to a VP and to a peer should feel like two completely different emails. This guide explains how to calibrate tone, length, formality, and structure based on who's reading.

---

## Audience Calibration Table

| Recipient | Tone | Length | What They Want | What to Avoid |
|-----------|------|--------|---------------|---------------|
| CVP+ | Formal, concise, strategic | 3-5 sentences | Impact, alignment, decisions | Fluff, operational details, caveats |
| Sr. Director | Professional, direct | 5-8 sentences | Recommendation + supporting evidence | Options without a recommendation |
| Peers (cross-team leads) | Collaborative, casual-professional | Flexible | What affects them, dependencies | Your internal team drama |
| Team members | Warm, clear, actionable | Flexible | Specific tasks, deadlines, context | Strategic framing they can't act on |
| Partners | Professional, mutual-benefit framing | 5-10 sentences | Shared goals, joint value, easy next step | One-sided asks, internal jargon |
| External (speakers, vendors) | Formal, appreciative | 5-8 sentences | Clear ask, easy to say yes to | Assumptions about their availability |

## Tone-Specific Writing Rules

### For CVP+ Audience
- **Opening:** One sentence, no pleasantries. "I'm requesting approval for X."
- **Body:** Maximum 5 sentences. Bold the key number and the ask.
- **Close:** "Happy to discuss" — not "Let me know when you're free."
- **Why:** They read 100+ emails a day. You have 15 seconds of their attention. Every word must earn its place.

### For Sr. Director Audience
- **Opening:** Brief context + the point. "Following up on our conversation about X — I'd like to proceed with Option A."
- **Body:** Include the recommendation and the logic. 5-8 sentences is the sweet spot.
- **Close:** Suggest a specific next step. "Can we finalize this in our Thursday sync?"
- **Why:** Sr. Directors want to see that you've done the thinking. They don't want to do your analysis for you — they want to validate or redirect your recommendation.

### For Peer Audience
- **Opening:** Casual but professional. "Hey [Name], wanted to loop you in on X."
- **Body:** Focus only on what matters to them. Skip your internal context.
- **Close:** Be direct about what you need. "Can you confirm your team can support this by [date]?"
- **Why:** Peers have their own priorities. Respect their bandwidth by keeping it focused on the intersection of your work and theirs.

### For Team Member Audience
- **Opening:** Warm and direct. "Hi [Name], wanted to share context on X and what I need from you."
- **Body:** Include enough context for them to understand the "why" behind the task. Be specific about deliverables and deadlines.
- **Close:** "Let me know if you have questions or see any issues with this timeline."
- **Why:** Team members execute better when they understand the purpose. Give them the strategic context (briefly) alongside the tactical ask.

### For Partner Audience
- **Opening:** Reference the relationship. "Great connecting at [event]" or "Following up on our partnership discussion."
- **Body:** Frame everything as mutual benefit. "This would give your team X while helping us achieve Y."
- **Close:** Make the next step easy and specific. "Would a 30-minute call on [date] work to discuss?"
- **Why:** Partner relationships are long-term. Every email should reinforce that you're thinking about their success alongside yours.

### For External Audience (Speakers, Vendors)
- **Opening:** Professional and appreciative. "Thank you for your interest in X."
- **Body:** Be clear about what you're asking, what the opportunity is, and what's in it for them.
- **Close:** Provide multiple options for next steps. Don't assume their availability.
- **Why:** External contacts have no obligation to respond. Make it easy, clear, and attractive.

## Universal Email Rules (All Audiences)

1. **Subject line is a headline.** "[Action Needed] Budget Approval for Partner Program — Due Friday" not "Re: Question"
2. **First sentence is the point.** "I'm writing to request X" or "Quick update on X." Never "I hope this email finds you well."
3. **Bold the key info.** Leadership scans, they don't read. Bold the status, the ask, the deadline.
4. **One email, one topic.** Don't combine a budget ask with an event update.
5. **End with a clear next step.** What do you want the reader to DO?
6. **Keep it short.** If it's more than a screen on mobile, it should be a document with an email summary pointing to it.
