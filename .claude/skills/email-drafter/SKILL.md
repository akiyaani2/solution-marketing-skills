# Email Drafter

Drafts professional emails matched to the recipient's seniority, the situation type, and the urgency level. Every email has a clear purpose, a scannable structure, and an explicit next step.

## When to Activate

Trigger on ANY of these signals:
- Direct: "draft an email," "write a message to [person]," "help me email [leader]"
- Indirect: "how should I phrase this," "help me word this," "I need to communicate this"
- Implied: user shares context and mentions needing to send it to someone, or mentions a difficult conversation that will happen over email
- Adjacent: "I need to ask [leader] for [approval/budget/decision]" — the output is almost certainly an email

If the purpose isn't clear, ask: "Is this a status update, an approval request, an escalation, partner outreach, or a follow-up?"

## Step 2: Route by Audience

**Who is receiving this email?** This determines tone, length, and formality.

Load the full calibration from [references/tone-guide.md](references/tone-guide.md), then apply:

| Recipient | Tone | Length | Key Rule |
|-----------|------|--------|----------|
| CVP+ | Formal, concise | 3-5 sentences | Every word must earn its place. Lead with impact. |
| Sr. Director | Professional, direct | 5-8 sentences | Include your recommendation. Respect their time. |
| Peers | Collaborative, casual-pro | Flexible | Only include what affects them. Skip your internal context. |
| Team members | Warm, clear, actionable | Flexible | Give context + specific deliverable + deadline. |
| Partners | Professional, mutual-benefit | 5-10 sentences | Frame as partnership, never one-sided ask. |
| External | Formal, appreciative | 5-8 sentences | Clear ask. Easy to say yes to. |

If the user hasn't specified the recipient level, ask. An email to a VP and an email to a peer should feel like two different emails.

## Step 3: Generate

Apply these universal rules:

1. **Subject line is a headline.** "[Action Needed] Budget Approval for Partner Program — Due Friday" not "Re: Question." The subject line determines whether the email gets opened or skipped.
2. **First sentence is the point.** "I'm writing to request X" or "Quick update on X." Never "I hope this email finds you well." Leadership has no patience for pleasantries in work emails.
3. **Bold the key info.** Leadership scans, they don't read. Bold the status, the ask, the deadline. If someone skims only the bold text, they should get the core message.
4. **One email, one topic.** Don't combine a budget ask with an event update. If you have two topics, send two emails. Mixed-topic emails get partial responses or no response.
5. **End with a clear next step.** What do you want the reader to DO? "Please approve by Friday" or "FYI only — no action needed."
6. **Keep it short.** If it's more than one screen on mobile, it should be a document with a 3-line email pointing to it.

## Step 4: Deliver

Output the complete email including subject line. Then offer:
- "Want me to adjust the tone, shorten it, or change the audience level?"
- "Want me to add/remove the recommendation section?"
- "I can also prep a follow-up email for after the response comes in."

## Gotchas

- Never send an email without a clear purpose. "FYI" emails should be rare and explicitly labeled as informational.
- Escalation emails ALWAYS include your recommendation. Don't dump the problem — show you've analyzed the options and picked one.
- For approval emails, make it easy to say yes: include all info needed to decide in the email body itself. Don't force the reader to open an attachment to understand the ask.
- Follow-up emails should go out within 24 hours while context is fresh.
- Read the email from the recipient's perspective before finalizing. What would YOU want to see first?
- If unsure about tone, err on the side of more professional. You can always be more casual in the reply.
