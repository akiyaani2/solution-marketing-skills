# Executive Summary — The Pyramid Principle

The Minto Pyramid Principle is the gold standard for executive communication. It's the reason your summary should lead with the answer, not build up to it. Here's how it works and how to apply it.

---

## The Core Idea

Traditional writing goes: Context -> Analysis -> Conclusion.
Executive writing goes: **Conclusion -> Supporting Arguments -> Evidence.**

Why? Because executives read from the top down and stop when they've seen enough. If your conclusion is on page 3, most of your audience will never reach it. The Pyramid Principle puts the most important thing first, then layers in supporting detail for those who want it.

---

## The Pyramid Structure

```
              [ANSWER]
            /    |    \
      [Arg 1] [Arg 2] [Arg 3]
       / \      / \      / \
    [E1] [E2] [E3] [E4] [E5] [E6]
```

### Level 1: The Answer (Top of the Pyramid)
- One sentence that states your conclusion, recommendation, or key finding
- This is what you'd say if you had 10 seconds in an elevator
- Example: "We should increase the hackathon budget by 30% because registration pace is 2x last year and we're capacity-constrained."

### Level 2: Supporting Arguments (3-5 grouped logically)
- These are the reasons your answer is correct
- Group them logically: by theme, by timeline, by stakeholder, by priority
- Each argument should be independently compelling
- Example: "Registration is outpacing capacity" / "Partner co-investment is confirmed" / "Competitive programs are scaling up"

### Level 3: Evidence (Data, examples, analysis)
- Each supporting argument has 1-3 pieces of evidence
- This is where the numbers, quotes, and specifics go
- Example: "2,400 registrations in 48 hours vs. 1,100 at the same point last year"

### Level 4: Details Available on Request
- Not in the summary itself — but referenced
- "Full analysis available in [link]" or "See appendix for methodology"
- This respects the reader's time while showing you've done the work

---

## How to Build a Pyramid Summary

### Step 1: Start at the bottom
Write down everything you know. All the details, data, analysis. Don't organize yet.

### Step 2: Group into themes
Look for natural groupings. What are the 3-5 themes that emerge?

### Step 3: Write a headline for each group
Each headline should be a complete thought, not a label. "Registration exceeds target" not "Registration data."

### Step 4: Write the top-level answer
Now that you see the themes, what's the single sentence that ties them all together? That's your answer.

### Step 5: Write top-down
Present the answer first, then the headlines, then the evidence. Reverse the order you discovered it in.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Building up instead of leading down
- BAD: "We analyzed the data from Q3. The results showed several trends. Based on these trends, we recommend..."
- GOOD: "We recommend increasing budget 30%. Here's why: [3 reasons]."

### Mistake 2: Labels instead of headlines
- BAD: "Registration Update" / "Budget Status" / "Timeline"
- GOOD: "Registration hit 150% of target" / "Budget on track at 60% spent" / "All milestones green through Q4"

### Mistake 3: Too many arguments at Level 2
- If you have more than 5 supporting arguments, you haven't grouped them well enough
- Combine related points into a single argument with multiple pieces of evidence

### Mistake 4: Burying the ask
- The ask (what you need from the reader) is part of Level 1 — the answer
- "We should do X [answer] and I need your approval by Friday [ask]"

---

## The Elevator Test

Read your summary's first sentence out loud. If someone in an elevator could understand the key message from that sentence alone, your pyramid is solid. If they'd need to read more to "get it," restructure so the answer comes first.
